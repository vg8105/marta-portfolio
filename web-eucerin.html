PROJECT 2

Eucerin x Rita Pereira

A landing page concept for Anti-Pigment skincare campaign.

ROLE Solo designer
YEAR 2026
TOOLS Figma
TYPE Class Exercise

OVERVIEW

Eucerin's Facebook ad featuring Rita Pereira promised visible results in 12 weeks, but the existing product page wasn't built to convert. This concept landing page was designed to bridge that gap: a single-goal page that converts cold ad traffic into leads through a free sample request, guiding the user from curiosity to conviction to action without offering an exit.

THE PROBLEM

The ad made a promise the product page couldn't keep.

Eucerin's Anti Pigment campaign led with a bold claim — 75% reduction in dark spots in 12 weeks — endorsed by Rita Pereira. But clicking through landed users on a generic product catalogue page with no narrative continuity, no social proof, and no clear next action. The design challenge wasn't aesthetic, it was strategic: how do you take someone who just saw Rita Pereira's face on Facebook and move them, in a single scroll, from recognition to trust to action? No navigation menu, no links to other products, no exits — just a logical progression where each section answers the question the previous one raised.

IMAGE PENDING

Current Eucerin's Anti Pigment landing page

PROCESS

The product was never the hero.

The first version of the hero centered the product line — three bottles on a gradient, a generic headline, and a CTA that said "View Products". It looked clean, but it could have been any Eucerin page. The ad that brought users here featured Rita Pereira's face and a specific promise; landing on a page that erased both broke the continuity the brief required. The second version replaced the products with Rita, replaced the headline with the ad's own language, and changed the CTA to "Discover my routine".

Then the teacher reviewed it and identified a structural problem: the hero's CTA didn't align with the page's actual conversion goal — a free sample request. The A/B test replaced the hero CTA with "Request free sample", aligning the first thing the user sees with the only thing the page is trying to achieve.

OUTCOME

The page covers all 12 required elements without feeling like a checklist. The A/B test hypothesis: replacing "Discover my routine" with "Request free sample" in the hero would increase CTA click-through and form completion rate, because the user understands immediately what is being offered and what action is expected.

REFLECTION

The free sample mechanism was chosen deliberately. It reduces entry friction by asking for no purchase commitment, which matches where the user is in the funnel when they see a Facebook discovery ad. The target audience (women 25–55, interested in science-backed skincare, responsive to trusted public figures) is familiar with skincare routines but may not know the Anti-Pigment line yet — a free sample gets the product into their hands without asking them to commit. What I would test next is whether the routine section introduces a choice that stalls the same user. For cold traffic with one specific skin concern, a single recommended product might convert better than a full routine.
